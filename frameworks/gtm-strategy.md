# GTM Strategy Framework — B2B & SaaS

> A GTM strategy is not a launch plan. It is a theory about how your product reaches the right customers, creates value for them, and converts that value into sustainable revenue.

---

## The GTM Strategy Stack

GTM is built in layers. Each layer must be coherent with the one below it.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│      MARKET SELECTION           │  Who are we going after and why?
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│      POSITIONING                │  Why us, for this customer, right now?
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│      CHANNEL STRATEGY           │  How do we reach them?
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│      SALES MOTION               │  How do we convert them?
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│      PRICING & PACKAGING        │  How do we capture value?
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│      CUSTOMER SUCCESS           │  How do we retain and expand them?
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

## Layer 1: Market Selection

**The question:** Which segment do we go after first, and why?

Most GTM failures are market selection failures. The product is fine; the company went after the wrong segment, or went after too many at once.

**How to select your initial market segment:**

Define the segment by 4 dimensions:
1. **Industry vertical** — not "B2B" but "logistics companies with 50–500 employees"
2. **Business motion** — are they growing, optimizing, or surviving? (Each requires a different message)
3. **Technology maturity** — what tools do they already use? What is their procurement process?
4. **Buying center** — who approves the purchase? Who influences it? Who blocks it?

**The beachhead principle:** Pick the segment where you can win clearly and completely, not the segment that is largest. Winning 40% of a $10M segment is better than 2% of a $200M segment — it creates a reference customer base you can expand from.

## Layer 2: Positioning

**The question:** Why should this customer choose us over every alternative — including doing nothing?

Positioning is not your tagline. It is the answer to the question: "In the mind of your target customer, what category does your product compete in, and why are you the best choice in that category?"

**The positioning statement structure:**

```
For [target customer],
who has [problem / unmet need],
[Product Name] is a [category]
that [key benefit / differentiation].
Unlike [primary alternative],
our product [key differentiator].
```

**Common positioning mistakes:**

- Positioning against a feature ("we have X, competitors don't") — features can be copied; positioning based on them is fragile
- Trying to be positioned for every possible buyer — positioning for everyone means nothing to anyone
- Confusing internal messaging with customer-facing positioning — customers don't care about your architecture; they care about their outcome

## Layer 3: Channel Strategy

**The question:** Where do your target customers discover and evaluate solutions like yours?

**Channel types and fit:**

| Channel | Best For | Investment Level |
|---|---|---|
| Outbound sales | High ACV ($10K+), enterprise, relationship-driven | High (people) |
| Inbound / content | Complex problems where buyers self-educate | Medium (time) |
| Product-led (PLG) | Low ACV, viral potential, self-serve value | Medium (product) |
| Partner / reseller | Fragmented markets, regional expansion, bundled offerings | Medium (ops) |
| Paid acquisition | Clear ROI path, measurable funnel | Variable (budget) |
| Community / events | Trust-building markets, technical buyers, MENA | Medium (time + budget) |

**Channel selection criteria:**
1. Where do your target buyers spend time and seek peer input?
2. What is your realistic CAC at each channel given your current stage?
3. Which channels are your competitors NOT owning? (Underserved channels have better economics)

**MENA-specific considerations:** In MENA enterprise markets, relationship-driven channels and events carry disproportionate weight. A warm introduction from a trusted peer closes faster than any content marketing play.

## Layer 4: Sales Motion

**The question:** How does a prospect move from unaware to closed?

**The three primary B2B sales motions:**

**Self-Serve (PLG)** — users discover, try, and buy with minimal sales involvement. Works when: ACV < $5K, value is demonstrable in <30 minutes, decision-maker = end user.

**Sales-Assisted** — product-led discovery, sales closes. Works when: ACV $5K–$50K, multiple stakeholders, procurement process exists but isn't formal.

**Enterprise Sales** — relationship-driven, multi-stakeholder, formal procurement. Works when: ACV > $50K, compliance requirements, security review, legal negotiation.

**The buying committee map (B2B):**

| Role | Their Question | Your Job |
|---|---|---|
| Economic buyer | "What is the ROI and who is accountable for it?" | Build the business case |
| Technical buyer | "Will this work with our stack? Is it secure?" | Provide technical documentation and POC |
| Champion | "Will this make me look good?" | Give them the narrative to sell internally |
| End user | "Will this make my job easier?" | Demonstrate the workflow improvement |
| Procurement | "Does this meet our vendor requirements?" | Prepare compliance documentation early |

## Layer 5: Pricing & Packaging

**The question:** How do we structure the price so that it aligns with the value we create and scales with customer success?

**Pricing models by business type:**

| Model | Logic | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Per-seat | Price scales with team size | Collaboration tools, productivity software |
| Usage-based | Price scales with value consumed | APIs, infrastructure, transaction-based |
| Tiered / feature-gated | Price scales with feature access | SaaS products with clear upgrade triggers |
| Outcome-based | Price tied to a business result | High-trust, measurable outcomes |
| Flat-rate | Simplicity over optimization | Early-stage, or when buyer has budget certainty needs |

**The packaging question:** What goes in each tier?

Package by *job to be done*, not by features. The basic tier should solve the core job completely. The upgrade trigger should be a natural expansion of that job, not an artificial gate.

**Common packaging mistakes:**
- Putting table-stakes features in the paid tier (creates resentment, not revenue)
- Gating on metrics the customer doesn't control (e.g., data volume when they can't predict their data volume)
- Too many tiers — decision paralysis favors inaction

---

## Layer 6: Customer Success

**The question:** How do we ensure customers get value — and expand when they do?

In B2B SaaS, the revenue from an average customer is earned over their lifetime, not at the point of sale. The GTM motion doesn't end at close — it ends when the customer has achieved the outcome they bought for.

**The expansion playbook:**

1. **Define the success milestone.** What does success look like for this customer in 90 days? Make it specific and measurable. If you can't name it, neither can they.
2. **Instrument time-to-value.** How long does it take a new customer to reach the first value moment? This is your most important onboarding metric.
3. **Build the expansion trigger.** What usage pattern or outcome indicates readiness for expansion? Build an alert for it — don't wait for a QBR.
4. **Create the expansion narrative.** The expansion conversation is not "we have more features." It is "you've achieved X — here's the next step in your journey."

## GTM Metrics by Stage

| Stage | North Star Metric | Supporting Metrics |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-launch | ICP validation rate | Customer interviews completed, pilot conversion rate |
| Early traction | Time-to-first-revenue | Win rate, sales cycle length, channel CAC |
| Growth | Net Revenue Retention | Expansion rate, churn rate, CAC payback period |
| Scale | LTV:CAC ratio | NRR by segment, logo churn by vertical |
