# Product Leadership Framework

> The difference between a strong IC PM and a product leader is not experience — it's the scope of problems you own and the leverage you create through others.

---

## What Product Leadership Actually Means

Product leadership is not about being a more experienced PM. It's about shifting your unit of impact from features to systems, from delivery to direction, and from individual output to team output.

You are a product leader when:
- The most important thing you do is set the conditions for others to make good decisions, not make the decisions yourself
- Your influence extends beyond your direct team
- You own outcomes, not outputs — and you're accountable for both

---

## The 4 Domains of Product Leadership

### 1. Vision & Strategy

**Setting direction that is specific enough to guide daily decisions but broad enough to survive changing circumstances.**

A good product vision answers three questions:
1. *Who* are we building for, specifically?
2. *What* will their world look like when we've succeeded?
3. *Why* does this matter — to them and to us?

A bad product vision is a list of features or a mission statement that could apply to any company.

**Strategy is the set of choices that makes vision achievable.** It identifies:
- The bets you're making (and are NOT making)
- The sequence in which you'll pursue opportunities
- The assumptions that, if wrong, would change the plan

**Vision/strategy communication cadence:**
| Audience | Frequency | Format |
|---|---|---|
| Leadership | Quarterly | Strategy review — what changed, what didn't, why |
| Team | Monthly | Roadmap context session — connecting daily work to vision |
| Stakeholders | On change | Decision memo — what changed and the reasoning |
| Company | Semi-annual | All-hands narrative — where we're going and how it's going |

---

### 2. Team Development

**Building a team that improves without you.**

The highest-leverage thing a product leader does is make the people around them better. This means:

**Hiring for trajectory, not just credentials.** A PM who has grown 30% in 18 months at a hard company will outperform a PM with an impressive CV who hasn't been challenged.

**Creating conditions for ownership.** PMs who are told what to build ship slower and think less than PMs who are given a problem and trusted to solve it. Your job is to set the problem clearly, not the solution.

**Calibrated feedback.** Feedback that is only corrective is demoralizing. Feedback that is only positive is useless. The goal is calibration — helping people understand where they are, where they need to go, and what the gap looks like.

**The 4 questions I ask in every 1:1:**
1. What's the most important thing you're working on right now?
2. What's blocking you — and is it a real blocker or is it anxiety?
3. What decision did you make this week that you're unsure about?
4. What's one thing I could do differently that would help you?

---

### 3. Cross-Functional Alignment

**Making the whole organization move in the same direction without controlling it.**

Product leaders operate at the intersection of engineering, design, data, sales, marketing, and operations. Your influence is primarily lateral — you don't have authority over most of the people you depend on.

**The mechanics of cross-functional influence:**

*Make the context visible.* People align when they understand why. Share the "why" behind decisions, not just the decisions. Document your reasoning, not just your conclusions.

*Invest in the relationship before you need it.* The worst time to build trust with the sales lead is when you need them to delay a deal to accommodate a product change. Build the relationship continuously.

*Name the trade-offs explicitly.* Alignment breaks down most often at the trade-off layer. If you need engineering to slow down to reduce tech debt, say so clearly — don't obscure it in roadmap language. People can accept trade-offs they understand.

*Create shared accountability.* If only product owns the outcome, other functions become service providers. When eng, design, and data co-own an outcome, they make better decisions independently.

---

### 4. Delivery Excellence

**Shipping things that matter, on a cadence that creates momentum.**

Product leadership is not just strategy — you need to ship. Teams that don't ship lose credibility with the organization; teams that ship the wrong things waste it.

**The delivery principles I operate by:**

*Scope for outcomes, not features.* The question before every sprint is: "What is the smallest thing we can ship that tests whether our hypothesis is true?" This is different from "What can we finish?"

*Make progress visible.* Shipping in the dark erodes confidence. Weekly internal updates, even on incremental progress, build organizational trust.

*Protect the team from organizational noise.* Half the job of a product leader is absorbing the chaos (competing priorities, leadership pivots, stakeholder demands) and giving the team a stable environment to build in.

*Create a culture of learning from launches.* Teams that don't do post-launch reviews repeat mistakes. Make the retrospective a norm, not a blame session.

## Signals You're Growing as a Product Leader

**From PM to Lead PM:**
- You can articulate the product strategy in one sentence and others can repeat it back accurately
- Junior PMs on your team are making decisions you would have made, without asking you

**From Lead PM to Group PM / Director:**
- You can disagree with leadership and change their mind with data and framing
- Your product area has a coherent strategy that survives personnel changes
- Other functions come to you for input on decisions that aren't "product decisions"

**From Director to VP:**
- You're building the systems (processes, hiring bar, culture) that produce good product outcomes, not producing the outcomes yourself
- The team performs well during periods when you're less involved
- You're shaping the company's product philosophy, not just executing it

## Common Leadership Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | What It Looks Like | Root Cause |
|---|---|---|
| Vision vacuum | Team asks "what are we doing and why?" before every sprint | PM hasn't set clear direction, or sets it and doesn't reinforce it |
| Execution fixation | Leader is in every Jira ticket but doesn't know the company strategy | Discomfort with ambiguity; retreating to tasks you can control |
| Consensus paralysis | Every decision requires buy-in from 10 people | Fear of conflict; unclear decision rights |
| Metrics theater | Team reports metrics that look good but don't connect to outcomes | Choosing metrics for ease of measurement, not relevance |
| Hero PM | Leader solves every hard problem themselves | Doesn't trust team; hasn't invested in developing them |
